Chi-Chang Lee

I am currently a research assistant in the HCIS Lab at Yang Ming Chiao Tung University in Taiwan, working under the supervision of Prof. Yi-Ting Chen. Since July 2023, I have also been collaborating with the Improbable AI Lab, led by Prof. Pulkit Agrawal at Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT). I earned both my Bachelor’s and Master’s degrees in Engineering Science and Computer Science from National Taiwan University, where I worked closely with Prof. Yu Tsao and Prof. Hsin-Min Wang. Before 2023, my research primarily focused on task-aware front-end processing for downstream audio applications, with a particular emphasis on developing robust speech-to-text systems (References: ICLR'23 and ASRU'23).

Beyond the input modality for models, I developed a keen interest in understanding how high-dimensional information from the perception stage influences downstream decision-making, and how these two modules can collaborate effectively. I believe that physical interaction with AI will be a pivotal area of focus in the next phase of AI development. Consequently, in 2023, I shifted my research focus to sensorimotor learning. My current works now center on the following two areas:

  • Heuristic Harnessing and Robustness in DRL: In practice, DRL training with finite sample sizes hinders the derivation of ideal expectations typically required by theoretical standards. Under these constraints, achieving unbiased optimization—such as with potential-based reward shaping—becomes empirically infeasible. Traditional DRL approaches often rely on heuristic rewards to encourage survival and exploration in complex robotic environments. My recent work focuses on addressing the exploitation-exploration dilemma under finite sample conditions, enabling more accurate approximations of optimal policies and improving the robustness of heuristic selections—greatly reducing the effort required for tedious heuristic design. (References: NeurIPS'24 and ICRA'24)
  • Perceivability Awareness for Decision-Making: A perception model might converge to a local minimum with similar total loss levels; however, the location of errors can significantly impact planning decisions. For instance, in an autonomous vehicle's planning scenario, whether the errors occur in distant or proximal areas relative to the ego vehicle is crucial for effective planning. My early work focused on planning-aware optimization to ensure that perception model training converges to a local minimum that aligns with both perception and planning goals. In addition, I am currently working on a project in the HCIS Lab that explores how perceivability can be integrated into policy learning and how to minimize perception requirements for decision-making when the agent has access to only non-privileged information. (References: ICLR'24)
